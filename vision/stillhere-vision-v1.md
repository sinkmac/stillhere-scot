STILL HERE — VISION DOCUMENT
Strategic summary for funders, partners, and cultural institutions
Version 1.0 — April 2026

What it is

Still Here is a free public platform for family memory. It gives ordinary people the tools to understand the lives of those who came before them — and to leave a permanent, human record of those lives for the people who come after.

It is not genealogy software. It is not a memorial service. It is not a social network.

It is a place where the distance between generations becomes visible, and where that visibility prompts people to act — to record, to remember, to pass something forward.

The problem it solves

In fifty years, your family will know your grandmother existed. They will find her name on a record and her dates on a certificate. What they will not know is how she laughed, what she worried about, what her life felt like from the inside, or what the world looked like when she was young.

This is not a failure of love. It is a failure of infrastructure. Nobody built the tool that made it easy to capture those things while there was still time.

Still Here builds that tool.

Why it works as a utility

The platform's entry point is Same Sky — a free, frictionless web tool. A user enters their name, their birth year, an ancestor's name and birth year. In seconds they receive an epoch report: a set of emotional cards showing the temporal relationship between their life and their ancestor's life, anchored to real historical events.

The output is not a chart or a database record. It is a feeling. The moment a user reads "your grandmother was exactly your age in 1951, the year rationing finally ended in Britain" — they feel the distance collapse. That feeling is what makes them act.

After the report, Still Here invites them to give that ancestor a permanent page. A photo. A memory. A URL that will still exist in a hundred years.

The tool creates the emotion. The emotion creates the archive.

The tool sequence

Still Here launches with Same Sky and expands through a deliberate sequence of tools, each one a different door into the same emotional territory:

Same Sky — the founding tool. Two birth years, one epoch report, one permanent ancestor page.

The Living Version — the same engine applied to living relatives. When your gran was your age it was 1971. A conversation starter, not a memorial. The broadest possible audience.

The Family Chain — three generations entered simultaneously. The tool surfaces how long the window stays open when all three are alive at the same time, and when it closes.

The Scottish Epoch — the same engine drawing on Scottish history specifically. The clearances, the famines, the industrial Clyde, the referendum. Built for the diaspora audience — the millions of people worldwide with Scottish roots who feel that connection but have no infrastructure to explore it.

The Witness Calculator — what did your ancestor witness that you never will? The last empire. The last war where nobody knew the outcome in real time. A different emotional register — not loss, but inheritance.

The Letter — the highest emotional ceiling of any tool on the platform. The same data generates a single letter, written in period-appropriate voice, from the ancestor to the user. One page. Print-ready. The kind of thing people frame.

Each tool adds new users, new SEO surface, new emotional entry points. All of them feed the same archive underneath.

What the archive becomes

Every ancestor page created on Still Here is a permanent public record. A name. A birth year. A photograph if the family has one. A memory in someone's own words. An epoch report showing the weight of that life against history.

Over time — across thousands of families, across the Scottish diaspora, across palliative care programmes and school projects and Sunday afternoons — the archive becomes something significant. Not a genealogy database. Not a government record. Something warmer and more human than either: a distributed monument to ordinary lives, built by the people who loved them.

This is the archive that the National Library of Scotland does not have. That no institution currently has. Because no institution has built the tool that makes contributing to it feel like something other than a chore.

The civic and palliative care dimension

Still Here has a natural institutional life beyond its public utility.

In palliative care settings, the platform gives patients and their families a structured, dignified way to create a record while there is still time. Not a death notice. A life deposit. The tools are simple enough to use in a hospice room on a tablet. The output is permanent enough to matter in a hundred years.

In schools, The Classroom variant of the same engine gives history teachers a tool that makes the past feel immediate and personal. When Churchill was your age, it was 1890. When your great-grandmother was your age, the Somme was still three years away.

In community settings — church groups, local history societies, diaspora organisations — Still Here gives people a reason to gather and record together. St Andrew's Day. Burns Night. The anniversary of a school closure or a factory's last day.

Each of these is a programme, not just a feature. Each is fundable. Each extends the platform's reach into communities that would never find it through search alone.

What makes it distinct

From genealogy software: Still Here does not help you build a family tree. It helps you feel what the tree means. The distinction is everything — one is a research tool, the other is an emotional experience.

From memorial SaaS: Still Here is not activated by death. It is activated by curiosity. The Living Version tool is specifically designed to be used while everyone is still alive. The platform's premise is that the time to record is now, not after.

From oral history projects: Still Here does not require specialist equipment, professional interviewers, or institutional backing. It requires a name and a birth year. Everything else is optional. The barrier to entry is lower than any comparable project in the field.

From social networks: Still Here has no feed, no followers, no engagement metrics. A page on Still Here is not a profile. It is a record. The difference in design intent produces a completely different experience — quieter, more permanent, more serious.

Why Scotland

Scotland has a disproportionate diaspora — an estimated 40 to 50 million people worldwide claim Scottish descent. That diaspora has deep emotional investment in ancestry and origin, disposable income, and almost no digital infrastructure that speaks to them in a voice that feels like home.

Scotland also has a civic culture that takes memory seriously — the Gaelic tradition of oral history, the strong local archive network, the cultural institutions that understand why ordinary lives matter. Still Here fits that culture precisely.

Building from Kirriemuir, Angus — a small town with its own deep literary and civic history — is not incidental. It is the provenance that gives the platform authenticity. This is not a Silicon Valley product that happens to have a Scottish theme. It is a Scottish product, built by people who understand what it means to be from somewhere.

The five phases

Phase 1 — Same Sky tool live at stillhere.scot. No database. Pure emotional tool.

Phase 2 — Permanent ancestor record pages. Photo upload. Family edit mechanic. The archive begins.

Phase 3 — The Living Version. Video testimony. Email delivery. The platform opens to living relatives.

Phase 4 — The Scottish Epoch, The Family Chain, The Witness Calculator. The tool suite grows. Diaspora and institutional audience develops.

Phase 5 — Civic partnerships. Creative Scotland. NLS. Palliative care organisations. Schools programme. The platform becomes infrastructure.

What we are asking for

At the institutional pitch stage, Still Here will be seeking partnership rather than funding in the first instance — specifically:

A named archive partner willing to receive and preserve Still Here records in perpetuity if the platform ever changes significantly. This is the preservation promise that makes the platform trustworthy to the families who use it.

A palliative care partner willing to pilot the platform in a hospice or care home setting and provide feedback on the testimony tools before Phase 3 launch.

A cultural partner — NLS, a local authority archive, a diaspora organisation — willing to co-present the platform at its civic launch.

Revenue comes from the archive's scale, not from its users. Advertising against high-intent genealogy and family memory search traffic. Institutional licensing for the schools programme. Grant funding for the civic phases. The platform is free to use. It always will be.
